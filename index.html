CHAPTER 1 — THEORY

IGA Fundamentals

Before touching OneIM, you must understand the field it operates in. Identity Governance and Administration (IGA) is a discipline — OneIM is its implementation.

🟢 Beginner — Essential Foundation

The Core Problem IGA Solves

In every organization, people join, move between departments, and eventually leave. Each person needs access to systems — email, file shares, business applications, HR systems, AD accounts, etc. Without a managed process, this creates serious problems:

⚠️
Access Sprawl
Employees accumulate more access than needed over time. When they move departments, old access is rarely removed — violating least-privilege principle.
🔍
No Visibility
IT can't answer "who has access to what?" — a basic question that becomes impossible without centralized tracking across dozens of systems.
📋
Compliance Risk
Regulations like SOX, GDPR, HIPAA require demonstrable control over who accessed sensitive data. Manual systems cannot produce this audit trail reliably.

IGA is the discipline that solves these problems — by providing a central platform to govern identities, manage access lifecycle, enforce policies, and produce compliance evidence.

Why "manual access management" always fails at scale

It's worth understanding why spreadsheets and ticket queues break down, because this is the exact pain a OneIM project is sold to fix. Picture a company of 5,000 employees with 40 connected applications. That's potentially 200,000 access relationships to track. People change jobs roughly every 18–24 months, each change touching dozens of entitlements. Manual processes fail for four structural reasons:

  • Volume: The number of access decisions grows with people × systems, which quickly exceeds what any team can review by hand.
  • Decay: Access is almost always added on request but rarely removed — there's no natural trigger for cleanup, so privilege only accumulates ("privilege creep").
  • Invisibility: Knowledge of "who approved this and why" lives in email threads and people's heads, not in a queryable system — so it evaporates when staff leave.
  • No proof: When an auditor asks "show me every person who could read payroll last March, and who approved it," a manual shop simply cannot answer with evidence.

IGA vs IAM — What's the Difference?

IAM — Identity & Access Management
  • Focus: Authentication and authorization (who are you, what can you do right now)
  • Tools: Active Directory, Azure AD, Okta, LDAP, SSO solutions
  • Question it answers: "Can this user log in? Can they access this resource?"
  • Time horizon: Real-time, transactional
  • Primary users: IT infrastructure teams
  • Examples: SSO, MFA, Active Directory
IGA — Identity Governance & Administration
  • Focus: Governance, lifecycle, compliance, visibility over access
  • Tools: OneIM, SailPoint, Saviynt, IBM ISIM
  • Question it answers: "Should this user have this access? Who approved it? Is it still needed?"
  • Time horizon: Lifecycle-oriented, process-driven
  • Primary users: IT security, audit, HR, compliance teams
  • Examples: OneIM, access reviews, provisioning workflows
💡
Key insight: OneIM is IGA, but it integrates WITH IAM systems (like Active Directory). OneIM reads from AD (synchronization) and writes back to AD (provisioning) — AD is a target system that OneIM governs, not a competitor to it.

Where PAM fits in the picture

You'll often hear a third acronym: PAM — Privileged Access Management. If IAM is "everyday login" and IGA is "governance over who should have what," PAM is the locked vault around the most dangerous accounts — domain admins, root, database sa. PAM tools (like One Identity Safeguard) check out privileged credentials just-in-time, record sessions, and rotate passwords. OneIM integrates with PAM through its Privileged Account Governance module so that even vault access is requested, approved, and attested like any other entitlement. Knowing the IAM / IGA / PAM distinction cleanly is a very common interview question.

The Identity Lifecycle — Joiner, Mover, Leaver

The most important concept in IGA is the identity lifecycle. Every identity goes through these stages:

  1. J
    Joiner — New Employee Arrives
    HR creates a record in the HRIS. OneIM reads this (via HR connector or CSV import). A Person record is created. Based on the person's department, job title, and location, business rules automatically determine which accounts to create and which roles to assign. The Job Server provisions these accounts in AD, Exchange, SAP, etc. — automatically, consistently, and auditably.
    Why it matters: A clean joiner process means a new hire is productive on day one with exactly the right access — no "I can't open my email" tickets, and no over-provisioning "just copy what Sarah has," which is how privilege sprawl starts.
  2. M
    Mover — Employee Changes Role
    HR updates the employee's department or job title. OneIM detects the change. New role entitlements are calculated and provisioned. Old entitlements that don't apply to the new role are revoked. This happens automatically — the user doesn't need to submit a ticket, and IT doesn't need to remember to do it manually.
    The hard part: Movers are where most real-world IGA programs struggle, because removing the old department's access is politically and technically harder than adding the new. OneIM's role model is what makes automatic revocation reliable — when the old Business Role is unassigned, its entitlements leave with it.
  3. L
    Leaver — Employee Departs
    HR marks the employee as terminated. OneIM immediately — or on the effective date — disables all accounts across all connected target systems, removes all group memberships, revokes all entitlements, and creates an audit record. No account is forgotten, no access lingers. This is a critical security control.
    Security stakes: The classic breach scenario is a disgruntled ex-employee whose accounts were never disabled. A reliable leaver process is often the single biggest security win a OneIM deployment delivers, and it's the first thing auditors test.
⚠️
Soft delete, not hard delete. A well-designed leaver process disables accounts rather than deleting them outright. Deleting an AD account destroys its SID, which breaks file ownership and audit history. The standard pattern is: disable immediately → remove group memberships → move to a "Disabled Users" OU → delete only after a retention period (e.g., 90 days).

★ Key Takeaways

  • IGA exists because manual access management fails on four fronts: volume, decay, invisibility, and lack of proof.
  • IAM = authentication now · IGA = governance over time · PAM = the vault for privileged accounts.
  • An Identity (Person) is not an Account — one person can own many accounts across many systems.
  • The Joiner / Mover / Leaver lifecycle is the spine of everything OneIM does. Movers and Leavers are where access cleanup happens.
  • Always disable rather than delete on departure — preserve the SID and the audit trail.
CHAPTER 2 — PLATFORM

What is One Identity Manager?

One Identity Manager (OneIM) is Quest Software's enterprise IGA platform. It is one of the leading IGA solutions in the market alongside SailPoint IdentityIQ and Saviynt.

🟢 Beginner — Essential Foundation

Platform Overview

OneIM is a modular, database-centric IGA platform that manages identity lifecycle, automated provisioning, access request workflows, compliance, and governance across the enterprise. It is installed on-premises (or in cloud VMs), with a central SQL Server database as its heart.

Unlike cloud-only SaaS IGA tools, OneIM runs in your infrastructure — giving you full control, deep customizability, and the ability to handle extremely complex enterprise requirements.

Three architectural choices define OneIM's personality and explain almost every design decision you'll meet later:

  • Database-centric: The SQL Server database isn't just storage — it holds the data, the configuration, the compiled business logic, and the work queue. Most of the "application" literally lives inside the database.
  • Modular: You license and install only the modules you need (Active Directory, SAP, IT Shop, Attestation, etc.). Each module adds tables, processes, and default content to the schema.
  • Object-oriented over SQL: You rarely write raw SQL against it. Instead you work with objects (Person, ADSAccount, Org) through an object layer that enforces rules, fires events, and writes to the queue for you.

OneIM Key Concepts — The Vocabulary

👤
Person (Identity)
CORE OBJECT · Central Identity Record

The Person object is the central identity record in OneIM. It represents a real human being — an employee, contractor, service account owner, etc. Person records are the source of truth for identity data. They are typically populated from an HR system or Active Directory import.

A Person is NOT the same as an account. A person can have multiple accounts across multiple systems — one AD account, one SAP account, one Exchange mailbox. All these accounts are linked back to the Person record. This linkage is what makes automated provisioning and de-provisioning possible.

Going deeper: Every Person has a CentralAccount (the canonical username, e.g. jdoe) and a CentralPassword that can be propagated to all linked target-system accounts. Persons also carry a state — internal vs external, active vs deactivated — and these state flags are exactly what the Joiner/Mover/Leaver processes read and write.

🗂️
DBQueue — The Heart of Async Processing
CORE MECHANISM · Task Queue · Job Dispatch

When you change any object in OneIM (create a person, assign a role, approve a request), it doesn't immediately go and write to AD or send an email. Instead, it creates an entry in the DBQueue — a table in the SQL database that acts as a task queue.

The Job Server (One Identity Manager Service) continuously polls this queue, picks up tasks, and executes them asynchronously. This architecture makes the system resilient (tasks don't get lost), scalable (multiple Job Servers can share the load), and auditable (every task is logged).

The chain reaction: One change can cascade. Assigning a Business Role writes one DBQueue task, whose calculation discovers three AD groups to provision, each spawning its own task. This is why a single click in Manager can produce a flurry of activity in the Job Queue — and why understanding the DBQueue is the key to debugging "why did/didn't this happen?"

🏢
Business Roles
CORE CONCEPT · RBAC Container · Provisioning Trigger

Business Roles are how OneIM implements Role-Based Access Control (RBAC). A Business Role is a container that groups together related entitlements (AD groups, SAP profiles, application permissions) that represent a business function (e.g., "Finance Analyst", "IT Administrator", "Sales Team Member").

When a Business Role is assigned to a Person, all the entitlements inside that role are automatically provisioned to the person's accounts. When the role is removed, the entitlements are revoked. This is the most powerful provisioning mechanism in OneIM.

Roles can nest and inherit. A "Finance — London" role can sit under a parent "Finance — All" role; members of the child automatically inherit the parent's entitlements. This lets you model an org chart once and let access flow down it, instead of re-listing the same groups in every role.

🛒
IT Shop (Service Catalog)
SELF-SERVICE PORTAL · Request Management · Approval Workflows

The IT Shop is OneIM's self-service access request portal. Think of it as an "Amazon for IT access." Service items in the catalog represent things users can request: software installations, AD group memberships, business roles, file share access, etc. When a user requests an item, an approval workflow is triggered. The approver(s) review and approve or reject. Upon approval, the access is automatically provisioned.

Why it complements roles: Business Roles cover standard, predictable access (everyone in Finance gets these groups). The IT Shop covers exceptions and extras — the things one person needs that aren't part of their role. Every request carries a reason, an approval trail, and optionally an expiry date, so even exceptions stay governed.

🔄
Synchronization
DATA INTEGRATION · Read from Target · Write to Target

Synchronization is the process that keeps OneIM's database in sync with external target systems (AD, Azure AD, SAP, etc.). It reads data from the target system and imports it into the OneIM database — creating or updating Person records, account records, group memberships, etc. It can also write changes back (provisioning). Each target system has a "Synchronization Project" configured in the Synchronization Editor tool.

Two directions, one project: The same sync project handles both inbound (reading AD into OneIM) and outbound (provisioning OneIM decisions back to AD). The mapping you configure once is reused in both directions, which is why getting the mappings right matters so much.

Step-by-Step: How a Provisioning Request Flows

  1. 1
    User Submits Access Request (Web Portal)
    User logs in to the Web Portal (Angular SPA in browser). They browse the IT Shop catalog and click "Request" on an AD group membership. The portal calls the API Server (or Application Server in older versions) via HTTPS REST.
  2. 2
    API/Application Server Processes the Request
    The Application Server authenticates the request (Windows auth, Forms auth, or OAuth). It applies security checks and business logic. It writes the new PersonWantsOrg record (the access request object) to the SQL Server database. An approval workflow entry is also created.
  3. 3
    DBQueue Entry Created Automatically
    The database write triggers a stored procedure that creates an entry in dbo.DBQueue. This entry specifies: which object changed, what task to run, and which Job Server queue to route it to. This is the core mechanism of all async processing in OneIM.
  4. 4
    Job Server Picks Up the Task
    The One Identity Manager Service (Job Server) continuously polls dbo.DBQueue. It picks up the task and executes the configured process — in this case, sending an approval email to the requester's manager.
  5. 5
    Approver Approves in Web Portal
    The manager receives an email with a link. They log in to the Web Portal, see the pending approval in "My Responsibilities → Approvals", and click Approve. This writes the approval decision back to the database.
  6. 6
    Provisioning to Active Directory
    The approval triggers another DBQueue entry — this time for the provisioning process. The Job Server picks this up, uses the Active Directory connector, connects to the AD domain controller via LDAP, and executes Add-ADGroupMember equivalent to add the user to the requested group. The access is now live in AD.
  7. 7
    Confirmation Email Sent
    A final DBQueue task fires the email notification process. The Job Server connects to the SMTP server and sends a "Your access request has been fulfilled" email to the requester. The entire end-to-end process is logged with timestamps in the database.
💡
Notice the pattern: Every step that does real-world work (email, AD write) goes through the same loop — write to DBQueue → Job Server picks it up → execute → log result. Once you internalize this single loop, the entire system stops feeling mysterious. "Nothing happened" almost always means "the task is sitting in the DBQueue and the Job Server isn't processing it."

High Availability & Scaling

In production, each tier scales independently — another benefit of the n-tier design:

  • Database: Protected with SQL Server Always On Availability Groups or failover clustering. There's one logical database, so its availability is paramount.
  • Job Servers: Run multiple Job Servers across servers. Each locks the DBQueue rows it takes, so they share load safely without doing the same task twice. You can also dedicate queues (e.g., one Job Server only does AD, another only sends email).
  • Application / API Servers: Run several behind a load balancer for both capacity and redundancy.
  • Web Portal: Being stateless against the App Server, it scales horizontally the same way.

Critical Architecture Rules

⚠️
Web Portal NEVER connects directly to SQL Server. The Web Portal always goes through the Application Server (or API Server). This is a security boundary. Only the Job Server and desktop clients (Manager, Designer) connect directly to SQL in standard setups.
💡
The database is not just a data store. The OneIM database contains compiled VB.NET assemblies, stored procedures, process definitions, scripts, and virtually all business logic. When you "compile" changes in the Designer, you are compiling .NET code INTO the database. This is a unique and important architectural characteristic.

★ Key Takeaways

  • OneIM is a four-tier system: Database → Service → Middle (IIS) → Presentation. Each tier talks only to its neighbors.
  • Every real-world action follows one loop: write to DBQueue → Job Server executes → log result.
  • The Web Portal never touches SQL directly — the Application/API Server is a hard security boundary.
  • The database holds data + config + compiled logic + queues, which is why its availability is the top priority.
  • Job Servers, App Servers, and the Web Portal all scale horizontally; the database is made highly available with Always On.

  • OrgHasADSGroup — role-to-group assignments
⚙️
One Identity Manager Service (Job Server)
WINDOWS SERVICE · Service Tier · Async Processing Engine

The Job Server is a Windows Service that runs in the background and processes all asynchronous tasks. It is the execution engine of OneIM. Without it running, nothing gets provisioned, no emails get sent, no sync jobs run.

It is configured via jobservice.cfg and registered in the OneIM database as a Server entry. Multiple Job Server instances can run simultaneously for load balancing and high availability — each processes tasks from its assigned queue name.

Server Functions matter: A Job Server only does what its assigned Server Functions (machine roles) permit. A running service with no functions assigned will silently process nothing — a classic "everything looks fine but nothing happens" trap covered in Chapter 9.

What It Does

  • Polls and processes DBQueue tasks
  • Executes provisioning to target systems
  • Sends email notifications (SMTP)
  • Runs synchronization jobs
  • Executes scheduled tasks & reports
  • Processes approval workflow steps
  • Runs compiled VB.NET process steps
  • Updates synchronization project data
🌐
Application Server
IIS WEB SERVICE · Middle Tier · REST/SOAP API Gateway

The Application Server is an IIS-hosted web service that acts as the bridge between web clients and the SQL database. All web-based clients (Web Portal, external API consumers) must go through it — they never connect directly to SQL. It handles session management, authentication, object caching, and enforces security.

It is configured via the Application Configurator tool and runs in its own IIS application pool with .NET 4.x Integrated Pipeline mode. It must be deployed and tested BEFORE the Web Portal can work.

Why caching here helps: The App Server keeps a cache of schema and frequently-read objects, so the portal feels fast and the database isn't hammered by repetitive reads. After certain config changes you may need to restart its application pool to clear that cache.

🛒
Web Portal (IT Shop Portal)
ANGULAR SPA · Presentation Tier · End-User Interface

The Web Portal is the browser-based self-service interface for end users. In OneIM 9+, it is a modern Angular single-page application. Users access it with a browser — no software installation needed. It connects to the API Server (not directly to SQL) for all operations.

Role-aware UI: What a user sees in the portal depends on their permissions. A plain employee sees the IT Shop and their own profile; a manager additionally sees "My Responsibilities" for their team; an attestor sees pending certification cases. The same portal serves all of them — the menu adapts to the logged-in identity.

Portal Modules

  • IT Shop — browse & request access
  • My Responsibilities — manage your team
  • Attestation — certify access reviews
  • Compliance — view SoD violations
  • Service Desk — create support tickets
  • Reports — run and view reports
  • Password Reset — self-service passwords
  • Admin area — system admin tasks
🖥️
Manager (Desktop Fat Client)
WINDOWS DESKTOP · Administration · Full Object Management

The Manager is the primary Windows desktop administration tool. It provides a rich tree-based UI for browsing and editing all objects in the system: persons, accounts, groups, roles, policies, jobs, reports. Most configuration tasks in the labs use the Manager. It connects directly to the database (or via Application Server).

Think of it as "SQL Server Management Studio but for OneIM objects." It is the most-used tool for day-to-day administrative tasks.

The Job Queue Info window inside Manager is where you'll spend much of your troubleshooting time — it shows every DBQueue/JobQueue task, its state, and its error message. Bookmark it mentally now.

🔬
Designer
CUSTOMIZATION TOOL · Schema Extension · Script Development

The Designer is the developer/customization tool. It allows you to: extend the database schema (add custom columns and tables), write and test VB.NET scripts, configure processes and events (automation triggers), define custom workflows, build report templates, manage system permissions, and create transport packages for migration between environments.

Manager vs Designer — the key distinction: Manager edits data (this person, this role). Designer edits the system itself (what a "person" even is, what happens when one is saved). Mixing them up is a common beginner confusion: you create a user in Manager, but you add a new field to all users in Designer.

⚠️
Changes in Designer affect the entire system and require a compilation step. Always develop in a test environment first. Mistakes in scripts can break entire processes. Lab IM-DSE-01 covers this safely.
🔄

The Synchronization Editor is used to create and manage synchronization projects — the configuration objects that define how a target system (AD, Azure AD, SAP, etc.) connects to OneIM. Each project specifies: which objects to sync, attribute mappings, merge rules (what to do when conflicts occur), provisioning rules (what to write back), and scheduling.

It is also where you test connectivity to target systems, run ad-hoc synchronizations, and review sync errors in detail.

Its logs are gold. When a sync misbehaves, the Sync Editor log names the exact object and exact attribute that failed — far more precise than the generic Job Queue error. Always read the Sync Editor log first for sync problems.

★ Key Takeaways

  • The database is central; if it's down, everything is down.
  • The Job Server executes everything async — but only for the Server Functions it's assigned.
  • The Application Server is the cached, secure gateway every web client must pass through.
  • Manager edits data; Designer edits the system. Don't confuse the two.
  • For problems: Job Queue (Manager) for general tasks, Sync Editor logs for sync issues.
CHAPTER 5 — DATA MODEL

OneIM Data Model

Understanding the key objects and relationships in the OneIM database is essential for effective administration, scripting, and troubleshooting.

🟡 Intermediate — Important for Admins

Core Object Hierarchy

OneIM uses an object model where everything in the system is an object with properties. Objects are linked to other objects through foreign key relationships. The schema is defined in metadata tables and can be extended.

How to read OneIM table and column names

The naming is remarkably consistent once you know the conventions — learn these five and you can guess most table contents:

UID_*
A foreign-key column holding a GUID that points to another table's primary key. UID_Person on ADSAccount means "the Person this account belongs to."
XHas* / *Has*
A many-to-many link (junction) table. OrgHasADSGroup links roles to groups; PersonInOrg links persons to roles.
XDate* / XUser*
System audit columns present on nearly every table — XDateInserted, XDateUpdated, XUserInserted record who changed what and when.
Is* / *State
Boolean flags (IsDisabled, IsExternal) and state columns (PWOState) that drive lifecycle logic.
CCC_ prefix
Reserved for your custom columns, so customizations never collide with future OneIM updates (covered in Chapter 16).

💡
The XOrigin column is a hidden gem. On membership tables like ADSAccountInADSGroup, XOrigin records why a membership exists — direct assignment, via a Business Role, via an IT Shop request, or via a dynamic rule. When you're asked "why does this user have this group?", XOrigin is the answer.

Key Relationships to Understand

💡
The core relationship chain: Person → PersonInOrg → Org (Business Role) → OrgHasADSGroup → ADSGroup → ADSAccount. This chain is what drives automated provisioning. When a Person is assigned to a Business Role that contains an AD Group, OneIM knows to add the person's AD account to that group.

Read the chain as a sentence: "This person is in this role, which has this group, so this person's account gets added to it." Each arrow is a row in a link table. If provisioning isn't happening, you debug by walking this chain link by link and finding the missing row.

Inheritance — The Engine Behind Roles

The reason roles are so powerful is inheritance calculation. OneIM continuously computes, for every person, the full set of entitlements they should have based on every role, dynamic rule, and request that applies to them. When something changes — a role is assigned, a parent role gains a group, a person's department changes — OneIM recalculates and queues exactly the additions and removals needed to make reality match. You don't tell OneIM "add this group"; you change an assignment, and inheritance figures out the consequences.

DBQueue — How It Works in Detail

  1. 1
    Object Change Triggers the Queue
    When any object is saved in the OneIM database (INSERT, UPDATE, DELETE), database triggers fire and evaluate which processes are configured to run for that event. If a matching process is found, a new row is written to dbo.DBQueue.
  2. 2
    Job Server Polls and Locks
    The Job Server polls DBQueue every few seconds. When it finds an unprocessed task, it locks the row (sets a processing flag) to prevent other Job Server instances from picking up the same task. This is how multi-instance load balancing works safely.
  3. 3
    Process Steps Execute
    The Job Server executes each step in the process definition. Steps can be: VB.NET script calls, connector operations (write to AD), external executable calls, email sends, or sub-process triggers.
  4. 4
    Task Completes or Errors
    On success, the DBQueue row is removed (or moved to archive). On failure, the ErrorCount is incremented and the task is retried (up to a configured limit). After max retries, the task stays in the queue with an error state visible in the Manager's Job Queue monitor.
⚠️
The DBQueue can back up. A mass change (e.g., importing 10,000 HR records at once) can flood the DBQueue with hundreds of thousands of tasks, and the system will appear "slow" while it drains. This is normal and self-healing — but it's why you stage large imports and monitor the queue depth. A permanently growing queue, however, signals a stuck Job Server or a task in an error loop.

Monitoring the Job Queue in Manager

SQL · SSMS
-- Check for errored DBQueue entries SELECT TOP 20 ObjectKey, TaskName, ErrorMessage, ErrorCount, StartTime FROM dbo.DBQueue WHERE ErrorCount > 0 ORDER BY StartTime DESC; -- Count tasks by state in JobQueue SELECT JobState, COUNT(*) AS Count FROM dbo.JobQueue GROUP BY JobState; -- Find slow-running processes SELECT TaskName, AVG(DATEDIFF(second, StartTime, FinishTime)) AS AvgSeconds FROM dbo.JobQueue WHERE JobState = 'Success' AND FinishTime IS NOT NULL GROUP BY TaskName ORDER BY AvgSeconds DESC; -- Watch overall queue depth (run repeatedly during a big import) SELECT COUNT(*) AS PendingTasks FROM dbo.DBQueue;
⚠️
Read, don't write. These queries are for observation only. Never UPDATE or DELETE rows in DBQueue/JobQueue directly — you'll corrupt the processing state. Use the Manager's Job Queue Info tools to re-enable or remove stuck tasks safely.

★ Key Takeaways

  • Naming is predictable: UID_ = foreign key, *Has*/*In* = link tables, CCC_ = your custom columns.
  • The provisioning chain is one readable sentence: Person → in Role → has Group → on Account.
  • Inheritance calculates entitlements automatically — you change assignments, OneIM derives the additions/removals.
  • XOrigin tells you why a membership exists — direct, role, request, or dynamic.
  • Observe the DBQueue with SQL, but never modify it directly — use Manager's tools.

CHAPTER 10 — INTEGRATION

Active Directory Synchronization

Connecting OneIM to Active Directory is the first major integration task. This is the foundation for all AD-based provisioning and user import capabilities. We'll connect to smplid.local from OIM01.

🗂️
Reference values used in this chapter: AD domain smplid.local (NetBIOS SMPLID), domain controller dc01.smplid.local, sync service account SMPLID\svc_oneim / SvcOneIM#2024.

What Synchronization Does

AD synchronization does two things: reads FROM AD INTO OneIM (importing users, groups, OUs into the OneIdentityManager database as Person, ADSAccount, and ADSGroup records), and writes FROM OneIM BACK TO AD (provisioning — e.g., adding a user to a group because a Business Role was assigned). Both directions are configured in a single Synchronization Project.

💡
Key vocabulary for sync projects: a connection defines how to reach the target; a schema is the map of object types and attributes the connector discovers; a mapping ties a target attribute to a OneIM column; a workflow bundles the steps of a sync run; and a matching rule decides whether an incoming object is "the same as" one already in OneIM. You'll meet each of these in the wizard below.

Step 0 — Prepare the AD Service Account (on DC01)

Before opening the Synchronization Editor, create a dedicated service account in Active Directory that OneIM will use to connect. Do this on DC01 using Active Directory Users and Computers, or PowerShell:

HR-Driven Identity Import (Real-World)

In real deployments, Persons are NOT created manually. They come from the HR system. OneIM supports HR imports via:

📄 CSV Import

🔌 Native HR Connectors

This separation is why a termination keyed in HR can cascade automatically into disabled accounts everywhere: HR flips one field, OneIM's leaver process does the rest. Designing the HR feed correctly is the highest-leverage decision in an IGA project.

Person Account Linking — How It Works

When AD synchronization imports an AD user, OneIM tries to match it to an existing Person using the matching criteria configured in the sync project. The default strategy:

  • Match by sAMAccountName = Person.CentralAccount — if a Person exists with a CentralAccount matching the AD username, the AD account is linked to that Person.
  • No match found? A new Person record is created automatically from the AD attributes (name, department, title, etc.).
  • Multiple matches? The sync project logs a conflict and does not link automatically — manual resolution needed.

Account Definitions — How Accounts Get Created Automatically

Beyond linking existing accounts, OneIM can create them. An Account Definition is a template that says "persons who qualify for this should have an account of this type, named by these rules, in this container." When a person gains an account definition (directly, via a role, or via the IT Shop), OneIM:

  • Generates the account name from a naming rule (e.g., first.last, with collision handling like john.doe2).
  • Applies a manage level (e.g., "Full managed" vs "Unmanaged") that controls how much OneIM governs the account's attributes.
  • Queues provisioning so the Job Server actually creates the account in the target system.
💡
This is the heart of joiner automation. A new HR record → person created → a dynamic rule grants a base account definition → the Job Server creates the AD account and mailbox → birthright groups follow. No human clicked "create user." Understanding account definitions is what separates someone who can use OneIM from someone who can design automation in it.

★ Key Takeaways

  • A Person is the identity; accounts are linked to it. CentralAccount is the canonical username and the default matching key.
  • In production, HR is the system of record — it owns who exists; OneIM owns what access that means.
  • Sync matching: one match links, no match creates, multiple matches flag a conflict.
  • Account Definitions are templates that auto-create accounts (naming rules + manage level) — the engine of joiner automation.
  • The IsExternal flag drives many lifecycle, attestation, and reporting rules — set it right.

Business Roles & RBAC

Business Roles are the cornerstone of automated provisioning in OneIM. Master this concept and you'll understand how access governance works at scale.

🟡 Intermediate — Core Concept

The Business Role Model

The idea is simple: instead of assigning individual permissions to each person, you define roles that represent job functions, put permissions in those roles, and assign the roles to people. When the role is assigned, all permissions provision automatically. When removed, they are revoked.

💡
Example: A "Finance Analyst" Business Role contains: AD group Finance-Team, AD group Finance-Reports-RO, SAP role FI_READ, and a software license for Excel Advanced. Assign this role to any person, and all four entitlements are provisioned automatically. Remove the role, and all four are revoked simultaneously.

Business Roles vs Application Roles vs Org Structures

OneIM actually offers several "role-like" containers under the umbrella of the Org table. Knowing which to use is a frequent design decision:

Department / Location / Cost CenterThe official org hierarchy from HRBirthright access by where someone sits in the org.
Application RoleAccess within one applicationBundling the entitlements that make up one app's "power user".
Dynamic RoleA rule-defined membership"Everyone whose Department = Finance" — membership computed, not assigned.

Creating a Business Role — Step by Step

  1. 1
    Navigate to Business Roles

  2. 2
    Add Entitlements to the Role
    On the Business Role's detail page, go to the Active Directory → Groups tab. Click Assign. Select the AD groups that members of this role should receive. You can also add SAP roles, IT Shop items, etc. from their respective tabs.
  3. 3
    Assign the Role to a Person
    On the Person record, go to the Business Roles tab. Click Assign. Select the Business Role. Save the Person. Watch the Job Queue — you should see tasks appearing to provision the AD group memberships.
  4. 4
    Verify Provisioning in AD
    After the Job Server processes the tasks (watch the Job Queue in Manager for completion), check Active Directory — the person's AD account should now be a member of the assigned groups.

Role Mining — Where Do Roles Come From?

The hardest part of RBAC isn't the tooling — it's deciding what the roles should be. Role mining is the analysis process of looking at who currently has what access and clustering it into sensible roles. Two complementary approaches:

  • Top-down: Start from the org chart and job descriptions. "What should a Finance Analyst have?" Clean, but can miss real-world access people actually need.
  • Bottom-up: Analyze existing entitlements and find patterns — "85% of Finance staff share these five groups, so they belong in a role." Reflects reality, but can bake in existing over-provisioning.
⚠️
Avoid "role explosion." A classic failure is creating so many narrow roles that you end up with more roles than users — at which point roles add complexity instead of removing it. Aim for a manageable set of broad birthright roles plus targeted IT Shop items for the long tail of exceptions. Good role design is iterative; you'll refine it with attestation data over time.

★ Key Takeaways

  • Business Roles bundle entitlements behind a job function — assign the role, access provisions; remove it, access is revoked.
  • Roles nest and inherit; pick the right container (Business Role vs Department vs Application Role vs Dynamic Role) for the job.
  • Four assignment paths: direct, indirect (role), IT Shop, dynamic — prefer indirect/dynamic for standard access.
  • Role mining (top-down + bottom-up) defines the roles; beware role explosion.
  • Always watch the Job Queue to confirm a role assignment actually provisioned.

IT Shop & Approval Workflows

The IT Shop is how end users request non-standard access in a controlled, auditable, self-service way. Setting it up correctly is one of the most important configuration tasks in OneIM.

IT Shop Structure

The IT Shop is organized into a catalog hierarchy:

  • IT Shop (root): The top-level container
  • Shelf (Regal): A category of services (e.g., "Software", "AD Groups", "Business Applications")
  • Service Item: A specific thing that can be requested (e.g., "Install Adobe Acrobat", "Join Finance-Reports AD Group")
  • Product (AccProduct): The actual entitlement the service item grants when approved (an AD group, a Business Role, etc.)
💡
Shops, shelves, and customers. Above the shelf sits a Shop (or "shopping center"), and every requester is a customer assigned to it via a customer node. This structure decides who can see and request what: a contractor's shop might expose only a basic shelf, while an IT admin's shop exposes privileged items. Designing the shop/customer assignment is as important as the items themselves.

Creating a Service Item

  1. 1
    Navigate to IT Shop Configuration
    In Manager: IT Shop → IT Shop → Your Shop → Shelves → [Target Shelf]. Click Assign Service Items. Or create a new AccProduct directly under IT Shop → Service Catalog → Service Items.
  2. 2
    Define What Is Granted (AccProduct)
    Link the service item to the Business Role, AD Group, or other entitlement that will be granted when approved. This is done in the Assignments tab of the service item — set the associated UID_AccProduct.
  3. 3
    Configure the Approval Policy
    Set which approval policy applies to this service item. The approval policy defines the workflow: single approver, multi-level approval, auto-approve, etc. You configure approval policies under IT Shop → Approval Policies.
  4. 4
    Test the Request in Web Portal
    Log in to the Web Portal as a test user (not viadmin). Browse to IT Shop → Request. Find the service item. Submit a request. Then log in as the approver and approve it. Verify the provisioning happens in the Job Queue.

How Approval Workflows Are Built — Policies, Workflows & Procedures

OneIM separates approval into three layers, which trips up beginners. Understanding the split makes the whole engine click:

LayerWhat It DefinesExample
Approval PolicyWhich workflow applies to a given request, and overall settings."Standard access policy" → uses the "Manager then Owner" workflow.
Approval WorkflowThe ordered chain of approval levels (steps).Level 1: line manager · Level 2: resource owner.
Approval ProcedureHow each level finds who the approver is.OM = manager of requester · OR = owner of the product.
💡
Approval procedures are little queries. Each procedure is essentially a rule that returns the set of people allowed to approve a given step — "the requester's manager", "members of role X", "the product's owner". OneIM ships many built-in procedures, and you can write custom ones. This is what lets a single workflow route correctly for thousands of different requesters automatically.

Approval Workflow Types

Auto-ApproveRequests are automatically approved with no human interaction. Useful for self-service items with no risk.Standard software, low-risk access
Single ApproverRequest goes to one defined approver (role owner, manager, or named person).Team applications, group memberships
Multi-LevelRequest must be approved sequentially by multiple approvers (e.g., manager → IT security → CISO).High-privilege access, compliance-sensitive systems

Governance Features That Make the IT Shop "Governed"

  • Validity / expiry: A request can carry a ValidUntil date so temporary access self-revokes — no cleanup ticket needed.
  • Mitigating questions & justification: High-risk items can force the requester to enter a business reason, captured in the audit trail.
  • SoD checks at request time: If granting an item would breach a Segregation-of-Duties rule, the request can be blocked or routed to an exception approver (Chapter 14).
  • Escalation & reminders: Pending approvals send reminders and can escalate to a deputy after a timeout, so requests don't stall forever.

★ Key Takeaways

  • Catalog hierarchy: Shop → Shelf → Service Item → Product (AccProduct); customer assignment controls who sees what.
  • Approvals are three layers: Policy (which workflow) → Workflow (the levels) → Procedure (who approves each level).
  • Workflow shapes include auto, single, multi-level, parallel, and delegated.
  • The IT Shop is "governed" because every request carries reason, approval trail, optional expiry, and SoD checks.
  • Use Business Roles for standard access, the IT Shop for exceptions and time-boxed access.

Compliance & Attestation

Compliance features are what make OneIM a true IGA solution (not just a provisioning tool). They provide the evidence and controls required by SOX, GDPR, HIPAA, and other regulations.

🟡 Intermediate — Advanced Admin

Attestation (Access Certification)

Attestation is the process where resource owners or managers periodically review and certify that access assignments are still appropriate. OneIM automates the entire process:

  1. 1
    Create an Attestation Policy
    In Manager: Attestation → Attestation Policies → New. Define: what to attest (Person entitlements, AD group memberships, Business Role assignments, etc.), the schedule (quarterly, annually), who are the attestors (managers, role owners, custom), and what happens on no-response (auto-revoke or escalate).
  2. 2
    Run the Attestation Campaign
    The Job Server triggers the campaign on schedule (or manually). It creates attestation cases — one per access assignment that needs to be reviewed. Attestors receive email notifications with links to the Web Portal to review.
  3. 3
    Attestors Approve or Reject
    Attestors log in to the Web Portal → Attestation → My Attestation Cases. They review each case and click Approve (access is still needed) or Deny (access should be removed). Denials automatically trigger revocation processes.
  4. 4
    Audit Report Generation
    OneIM generates attestation completion reports showing: percentage reviewed, approved vs rejected, who reviewed what, and when. These reports are the compliance evidence required by auditors.
💡
Why "deny" must actually remove access. The power of attestation is that a denial isn't just a note — it fires a revocation process that removes the entitlement for real. This closed loop ("review → decision → enforcement") is exactly what auditors look for. A review system where "deny" doesn't remove anything is just paperwork.

Segregation of Duties (SoD)

SoD rules prevent one person from holding combinations of access rights that could enable fraud or abuse. Example: a person cannot be both a Payment Creator AND a Payment Approver in SAP.

  • Define SoD Rules: Manager → Compliance → Compliance Rules → New. Select the conflicting entitlement pairs.
  • Evaluation runs automatically: When access changes are made, OneIM evaluates SoD rules and creates Compliance Violations if conflicts are detected.
  • Violations appear in the portal: The Web Portal's Compliance module shows violations. Mitigating controls can be documented. Unmitigated violations can block access requests.
  • Risk scoring: Each rule can have a risk score. High-risk violations escalate to security review.

Preventive vs Detective Controls

SoD operates in two timings, and good designs use both:

ControlWhen It ActsEffect
PreventiveAt request time, before access is granted.Blocks the conflicting request outright, or forces it down an exception-approval path with extra sign-off.
DetectiveOn a schedule, scanning access that already exists.Surfaces violations that crept in (e.g., via direct AD changes outside OneIM) so they can be remediated.
⚖️
Mitigating controls are a first-class concept. Sometimes a genuine business need requires a person to hold a conflicting pair (a tiny team where one person must do two jobs). OneIM lets you record a documented mitigating control ("all their transactions are reviewed monthly by the controller") and attach it to the violation. The violation still shows, but it's flagged as accepted-with-mitigation — which is exactly what an auditor wants to see, rather than the conflict being silently ignored.

Risk Index — Quantifying Access Risk

OneIM can attach a risk index (a number, often 0–1) to entitlements, roles, and SoD rules. It then rolls these up per person to answer "who are our riskiest identities?" This lets you:

  • Prioritize attestation — review the highest-risk access more often.
  • Trigger extra approval steps automatically when a request pushes someone over a risk threshold.
  • Report risk trends to leadership in business terms, not raw entitlement counts.

★ Key Takeaways

  • Attestation = scheduled "is this access still needed?" reviews; a deny actually revokes, closing the loop.
  • SoD rules block toxic combinations (e.g., create + approve payments) and raise violations.
  • Use both preventive (block at request) and detective (scan existing) controls.
  • Mitigating controls let you accept a necessary conflict with documented compensating oversight.
  • The risk index turns access into a number you can prioritize and report on.

VB.NET Scripting in OneIM

OneIM's scripting engine uses VB.NET. Scripts live in the database, are compiled at runtime by the Job Server, and can interact with any object in the system. This is what makes OneIM infinitely customizable.

🔴 Advanced — Developer Level

Scripting Environments

Scripts in OneIM can run in different contexts:

Value Calculations
Scripts that dynamically calculate a property value. Example: auto-generate an AD account name from FirstName + LastName rules.
Condition Scripts
Scripts that return true/false to determine if a process step should execute or if a rule applies. Example: only run if department = "Finance".
Process Step Scripts
Scripts that execute as part of a Process — the main action logic. Example: make an API call to an external system, transform data, execute complex business logic.
Event Handler Scripts
Scripts that run before or after an object is saved. Example: validate data before insert, audit log on change.

Scripting API — Key Objects

In OneIM scripts, the following objects are available:

VB.NET — OneIM Scripting API
' The current object being processed Dim person As IEntity = Session.Source() ' Reading a property value Dim firstName As String = person.GetValue("FirstName").String ' Setting a property value person.PutValue("CentralAccount", firstName.ToLower() & lastName.ToLower()) ' Load an object by primary key Dim conn As IConnection = Session.GetConnection() Dim qry As New SqlQuery("ADSAccount") qry.Where = "UID_Person = @uid" qry.AddParameter("@uid", person.GetValue("UID_Person").String) Dim adAccount As IEntity = conn.Get(qry) ' Log a message (visible in Job Queue logs) Session.LogMessage(LogSeverity.Info, "Processing person: " & firstName) ' Value calculation script - return a computed value Public Function ComputeAccountName() As String Dim src As IEntity = Session.Source() Dim first = src.GetValue("FirstName").String.ToLower() Dim last = src.GetValue("LastName").String.ToLower() Return first & "." & last End Function
💡
Base vs custom scripts. OneIM ships thousands of built-in scripts. The golden rule: never edit a base script directly — your change will be overwritten on the next upgrade. Instead, create a custom script (conventionally prefixed CCC_) and have it call or override the base behavior. The same discipline that protects custom columns protects custom code.

The #VI() Dollar / Value Expression Syntax

Alongside full scripts, OneIM uses short inline expressions you'll see constantly. The most important is the dollar ($) notation for walking object relationships:

VB.NET — $-notation property access
' Read a column on the current object Value = $FirstName$ ' Walk a foreign key: the department name of this person's manager Value = $FK(UID_PersonHead).Person.Department$ ' Common in process/condition scripts to make decisions If $IsExternal:Bool$ Then ' shorter validity period for contractors End If

The $Column$ form reads a value; $FK(...)...$ follows a foreign key to a related object. Recognizing this syntax is half the battle when reading existing OneIM customizations.

Where to Write Scripts

All scripts are written in the Designer. Navigate to:

  • Designer → Script Library — for reusable script functions that can be called from anywhere
  • Designer → Object Properties → [Object Type] → [Property] → Value Calculation — for property computation scripts
  • Designer → Processes → [Process] → [Step] → Script — for process step scripts
  • Designer → Object Events → [Event] → Script — for event handler scripts
⚠️
After writing any script in Designer: You must Compile (Ctrl+Shift+B or the compile menu) to push your changes to the database. Until you compile, your scripts are NOT active in the running system.
🧪
Test scripts safely with the Script Test Environment. The Designer includes a script tester where you can run a script against a chosen object and inspect the result without affecting live data. Use it before compiling — catching a logic error here is free; catching it after it's broken provisioning for 5,000 users is not.

★ Key Takeaways

  • OneIM scripts are VB.NET, stored in the DB and compiled by the Designer; they run in four contexts (value calc, condition, process step, event handler).
  • Core API: Session.Source(), GetValue/PutValue, SqlQuery, Session.LogMessage.
  • Learn the $Column$ and $FK(...)...$ expression syntax — it's everywhere.
  • Never edit base scripts — write CCC_ custom ones; always compile after changes.
  • Use the Script Test Environment to validate logic before it touches live data.

🔴 Advanced — Developer Level

Why Extend the Schema?

Clients often have business requirements that don't map to standard OneIM fields. Examples:

  • Adding a "CostCenter" field to Person that maps to an HR system field

  • Adding a "RiskScore" calculated field to evaluate user access risk
⚠️
Extend only when you must. Schema changes are permanent, must be carried through every environment via transport, and add maintenance weight. Before adding a column, check whether a standard field already fits, or whether a dynamic foreign key / existing relationship can model the need. The best customization is the one you didn't have to make.

  2. 2

  3. 3
    Compile and Deploy

  4. 4
    Make It Visible in Manager UI
    To show the new column in the Manager's Person form, navigate to Designer → UI Forms → Person → Person detail form. Add the new column to the appropriate form section. Compile and test in Manager.

Moving Changes Between Environments — Transport Packages

You never build customizations directly in production. Instead you develop in DEV, then carry the change forward as a transport package:

  • Database Transporter bundles your selected changes (schema, scripts, processes, config) into a single .zip transport file.
  • The same file is imported into TEST, then PROD, guaranteeing the three environments stay identical.
  • Each import is logged, giving you a repeatable, auditable promotion path — the OneIM equivalent of a software release.
⚠️
DEV → TEST → PROD, always in that order. A schema change applied straight to production with no test pass is the fastest way to cause an outage. Transports exist precisely so the exact change you validated in TEST is the one that lands in PROD — no manual re-clicking, no drift.

★ Key Takeaways

  • Extend the schema for genuine gaps only — prefer standard fields/relationships first.
  • Always use the CCC_ / CCCTable_ namespace so upgrades never clobber your work.
  • Adding a column = define in Designer → compile (runs ALTER TABLE) → add to the UI form.
  • Promote changes with transport packages through DEV → TEST → PROD, never directly in production.

Generating Conditions & Pre-Scripts — Controlling When a Process Runs

A process doesn't blindly run on every event. Two gates decide whether it actually fires and what data it carries:

  • Generating condition: A WHERE-style condition evaluated when the event fires. "Only generate this process if IsExternal = 1." If it's false, no DBQueue task is created at all.
  • Pre-script for generating: A script that runs before the process is generated, used to compute values and pass them as parameters into the process steps — so each step has exactly the data it needs.
💡
Process steps are typed by "function." Each step uses a component/function that determines what it does — ScriptComponent runs VB.NET, the AD connector functions write to Active Directory, MailComponent sends email, CommandComponent runs an executable. You build automation by chaining typed steps, each with its own parameters and success/error branches.

Creating a Custom Process

  1. 1
    Open Designer → Processes
    Navigate to Designer → Processes → [Object Type, e.g., Person]. Right-click → New Process. Give it a descriptive name and set the event it responds to (Insert, Update, etc.).
  2. 2
    Add Process Steps
    In the process definition, add steps. Each step has a Function (what type of action: VB script, mail send, connector operation, etc.) and Parameters. Steps execute in order, and you can set conditions on each step to control branching.
  3. 3
    Compile and Test
    Compile the Designer changes. Then trigger the event (e.g., update a Person record) and watch the Job Queue in Manager for the process to execute. Check the step logs for any errors.

Debugging Processes — The Process History

When a custom process misbehaves, OneIM gives you a frame-by-frame replay:

  • Process tracking / history shows every generated process, each step, its parameters, and its success/error result.
  • You can see exactly which step failed and the error it returned — far better than guessing.
  • Failed steps can often be re-enabled (retried) once you've fixed the root cause, without re-triggering the whole event.
⚠️
Enable process tracking deliberately. Detailed tracking is invaluable for debugging but generates volume, so it's often tuned per process rather than left maximal everywhere. When developing a new process, turn tracking up; once it's stable in production, dial it back to what audit requires.

★ Key Takeaways

  • The automation chain is Object change → Event → Process → DBQueue → Job Server runs Steps.
  • Events come in Insert/Update/Delete/Custom/Scheduled flavors.
  • Generating conditions decide whether a process runs; pre-scripts compute the data it runs with.
  • Steps are typed by function (script, AD connector, mail, command) and chained with success/error branches.
  • Debug with process history/tracking — it pinpoints the failing step; retry after fixing the cause.

OneIM has a sophisticated permissions model. Understanding it is essential for setting up multi-admin environments, restricting access to sensitive data, and passing audits.

OneIM Permission Model

Access to OneIM objects is controlled through System Roles (called Permission Groups in some versions) assigned to OneIM user accounts. This is separate from Windows/AD permissions.

System Administrator
viadmin — full access to everything. Never use for day-to-day operations in production. Only for initial setup and emergency access.
IT Administrators
Full access to the Manager for all objects. Standard admin role for the team managing the OneIM system.
Role Owners
Can manage their own Business Roles and IT Shop items. Cannot see other teams' data. Used for delegated role management.
End Users
Web Portal only. Can request access for themselves, manage their responsibilities, participate in attestations. No Manager access.

The Building Blocks — Permissions Groups & Program Functions

OneIM's internal security has its own vocabulary, distinct from AD:

ConceptWhat It Controls
Permissions GroupA bundle of table/column rights. Membership grants the ability to read/edit specific object types.
Program FunctionA named capability that gates a feature or action (e.g., "may run this report", "may export data"). Checked in code/UI.
System RoleA convenience container that bundles permissions groups (and other assignments) so you grant a job profile in one move.
Dynamic GroupPermission-group membership computed from a condition, so the right people get the right rights automatically.
💡
Row-level security via conditions. Beyond "can you edit the Person table at all," OneIM can scope which rows a group sees using conditions — e.g., "HR admins in the UK see only persons where Country = UK." This is how large organizations delegate administration regionally without exposing everyone's data to everyone.

Creating OneIM System Users

  1. 1
    Create a System User Account
    In Manager: System Configuration → Security → System Users → New. Set the username (usually matches the person's AD account), set the password, and link it to the Person record.
  2. 2
    Assign to a System Role (Permission Group)
    On the System User, go to the System Roles tab. Assign the appropriate role (e.g., "All persons (Person)" permission set for read access to person data, "IT Shop / Requestor" for Web Portal access).
  3. 3
    Enable Windows Authentication (Production)
    For production, configure Windows authentication so users log in to the Web Portal with their AD credentials (SSO). Configure this in the Application Server settings: authentication module = "Windows Authentication Provider".

Authentication Modules — How Users Prove Who They Are

OneIM doesn't hard-wire one login method; it uses pluggable authentication modules, and you can offer different ones to different tools:

  • System user (named): Username/password stored in OneIM. Used for viadmin and break-glass accounts.
  • Windows / Active Directory: Single sign-on with the user's logged-in AD identity — the usual choice for the Web Portal.
  • OAuth / OpenID Connect: Federated login via an external IdP (Entra ID, etc.), common in modern v9+ deployments.
  • Dynamic (role-based) login: The user is matched to a Person, and their effective permissions are derived from that Person's roles — so access in the tools mirrors their governed access.
🔐
Treat viadmin as break-glass. The built-in superuser should be locked away (strong password in a vault, usage logged) and used only for emergencies and initial setup. Daily work happens under named, least-privileged system users so every action is attributable to a real person — which is exactly what an auditor will check.

★ Key Takeaways

  • OneIM has its own permission model, separate from AD: Permissions Groups + Program Functions, bundled into System Roles.
  • Security can be row-level via conditions — delegate administration by region/department without oversharing.
  • Users authenticate through pluggable authentication modules (system user, Windows/AD SSO, OAuth/OIDC, dynamic).
  • viadmin is break-glass — vault it; do daily work as named least-privileged users for full attribution.

TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDE

Common Issues & Solutions

A systematic approach to diagnosing and fixing the most common OneIM problems encountered in labs and production environments.

Troubleshooting Framework

💡
Always start here: (1) Check the Job Queue in Manager for error tasks, (2) Check Windows Event Viewer on the OneIM server, (3) Check IIS logs if it's a web issue, (4) Check SQL Server error logs, (5) Check Sync Editor logs for sync issues.
🧭
The golden mental model: almost every "it didn't work" reduces to one question — did the task reach the DBQueue, and did the Job Server execute it? Work that one axis first. If the task isn't in the queue, the problem is upstream (config, condition, permissions). If it's in the queue but stuck, the problem is the Job Server (not running, wrong Server Functions). If it executed but errored, read the step error. This single diagnostic spine resolves the majority of cases below.

Issue: Job Server Not Starting

Symptoms: The "One Identity Manager Service" Windows service fails to start or stops immediately.

  • Check: Windows Event Viewer → Application Log. Look for "One Identity Manager Service" errors.
  • Check: Can the service account connect to SQL? Test with SSMS using the same credentials.
  • Check: Port 1433 — is SQL Server running? Test-NetConnection -Port 1433
  • Check: jobservice.cfg — is the connection string correct? Is the queue name unique?
  • Fix: If credentials changed, update them in jobservice.cfg and in the Service Properties (Log On tab) in services.msc

Issue: Web Portal Shows Blank Page or Error

  • Check: Is the Application Server working? Browse directly to http://server/AppServer/SessionService.asmx. If that fails, fix the App Server first.
  • Check: IIS Application Pool — is it running? Not stopped due to identity error?
  • Check: IIS logs at C:\inetpub\logs\LogFiles\ — what HTTP error code appears?
  • Check: The Web Portal's appSettings.json (or web.config) — is the Application Server URL correct?
  • Fix: Restart the IIS Application Pools for both App Server and Web Portal in IIS Manager.

Issue: Synchronization Fails

  • Check: Synchronization Editor → the sync project → View Logs. The log shows the exact error and which object caused it.
  • Check: Can the OneIM server reach the AD domain controller? Test-NetConnection -Port 389 to the DC.
  • Check: Service account permissions — does it have read access to the OU being synced?
  • Check: Is the Job Server running? Sync jobs are triggered and executed by the Job Server.
  • Check: The Job Queue in Manager — is there an error entry for the sync job?

Issue: Provisioning Not Happening (AD Group Not Assigned)

  • Check: Job Queue in Manager — is there a pending or errored provisioning task?
  • Check: Is the Job Server running? Get-Service "*OneIdentity*"
  • Check: Does the server entry in Manager have the "Active Directory" component assigned under Machine Roles?
  • Check: AD connector account — does it have write permission to modify group memberships in AD?
  • Check: The Business Role chain — is the role actually assigned to the person? Is the group assigned to the role? In Manager, trace: Person → Business Roles tab → [Role] → Groups tab → [Group].

Issue: Database Installer Fails

  • Check: DBInstaller log file (usually in %TEMP%\OneIdentity\) — read the exact error message.
  • Most common causes: SQL Agent not running, Full-Text Search not installed, insufficient disk space on SQL Server, network interruption during the 30-60 minute install.
  • Fix: Resolve the root cause. Drop the partially created database (DROP DATABASE OneIdentityManager in SSMS). Then re-run the installer from scratch. Never try to resume a partial install.

  • Fix problems at the earliest broken layer — don't debug the Portal if the App Server endpoint itself errors.
  • The right log for the job: Job Queue (tasks), Sync Editor log (sync), Event Viewer (service), IIS logs (web).
  • Keep Test-NetConnection for 1433 and 389 handy — connectivity is the most common silent culprit.
